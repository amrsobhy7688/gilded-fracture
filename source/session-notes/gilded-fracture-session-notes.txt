Gilded Fracture - Session Notes
Prologue: The Ledger and the Missing 46
In the year 643 post-Unmaking, your name is not written in ink. Your name is written in light.


To be a citizen of the Cyriath Empire in good standing is to exist inside a ledger so vast and so precise that the Ministry of Sanctity knows, within a margin of three percent, exactly how much aether your household consumed before you finished your morning tea.


The contribution of your life, your work, and your house, noble or common, to the Empire’s illuminated whole is your reputation, your credit, and your proof that you belong to the light rather than the darkness that preceded it.


Cyritium is the capital of that ledger. Where Lunaris mines, Solaris trades, and Cyritium accounts. The great houses of the city do not make things. They verify them. They authorize them. They stamp the seal that says:


This crystal is sanctified.
This shipment is legitimate.
This person is in good standing.
This light is holy.


The Celestial Ledger, maintained in the Office of Holy Order beneath the High Spire, is the longest unbroken document in the known world: 643 years of every crystal that has ever moved through Imperial hands, recorded in the precise, unhurried handwriting of a hundred generations of House Mccord auditors.


The Empire does not run on faith alone.


It runs on faith and receipts.


This is why Cyritium, in the fifteen days before Ascension Day, is the most orderly place in the world. Every house is preparing its quarterly yield reports. Every ministry contract is being reviewed and renewed. Every crystal destined for the festival decorations, the white silk-draped bridges, the great beacon at the High Spire peak, and the Everlight sconces lining the processional route from the Pilgrim Gate to the cathedral steps has been logged, sanctified, inspected, and assigned.


The city is a machine running at its annual peak.


The hum in the walls is warmer this week than any other. The Radiant Broadcast speaks of record yields and divine favor. The Herald’s voice carries that particular tone it only carries in Ascension Week.


Not triumphant.


Satisfied.


The tone of a system working precisely as it was designed to work.


You are, by any measure, exactly where you are supposed to be.


There is only one small thing.


Forty-six crystals are missing from the ledger.


A rounding error.
A clerical mistake.
The kind of thing that happens when you are operating at this scale.


Noted. Set aside. Not something any reasonable person would pursue when the Empire is in Festival Week, every house’s compliance report is due, the hum is warm, the silk is draped, the Radiant Broadcast is speaking, the law is absolute, the light is gold, and everything, by every account ever filed in 643 years of receipts, is exactly where it is supposed to be.
Leo at the Tea House
Fifteen days before Ascension Day, the morning opens in a small tea house.


The place is quiet at this hour. Most of the city is already at work, leaving only a few scattered patrons seated apart from one another. In a back corner, at a table tucked away from the room’s easy traffic, Leo sits across from an older woman.


Leo is twenty-three, composed, and dressed simply in a white shirt. He sits with a practiced stillness, quiet and difficult to read.


The woman across from him appears to be in her fifties, perhaps fifty-five. She has streaks of white in her dark hair and the controlled expression of someone accustomed to keeping herself composed. Her clothes mark her as connected to a noble house, likely a mid-tier functionary, though the quality of the garments is slightly too fine for the rank she appears to represent.


Her name is Felda.


A cup of tea sits untouched in front of her. It has already begun to cool.


She looks at Leo and says, “You said three days.”


Her tone makes it an accusation.


Leo answers, “Four.”


Felda studies him.


“Does this mean you haven’t started yet?”


“We have time,” Leo says. “I’ll give it to you tomorrow.”


“Do you notice things you are not paid to notice, Mr. Gray?”


“As?”


“It seems you are answering questions with questions. When I hire a private investigator, I expect answers.”


“I told you. All the answers will be with you tomorrow.”


Felda holds his gaze for a moment.


“I will put my faith in you this time because I have no other options.”


She lifts her cup, drinks, then turns the saucer and lets the remaining tea shift across it. From her pocket, she takes out an envelope and places it on the table. She pushes it toward Leo.


“Cash. Correct amount. No extra. I expect it done by tonight.”


Then she gathers her things and leaves.


Only after she is gone does Leo force himself to remember what this job was supposed to be.


Felda had hired him roughly ten days earlier. She wanted him to go to a specific government office, locate an original document, and switch it out. She wanted the original itself, not a copy. The exact document slips away from him, but he is certain he took notes somewhere in his apartment.


As Leo looks back over the table, he notices something beneath the saucer.


A small folded paper.


It had not been there when Felda arrived.


The way she tilted the saucer was not random. It was a signal. The note appears to have been passed to her, not written by her, and she left it behind deliberately.


Leo opens it.


The clerk is asking about the forty-sixth line again. It has been handled. Do not involve anyone else.


Felda is trying to involve him without openly involving him.


Leo leaves the tea house and returns to his apartment.


It takes him about half an hour to walk back. His apartment is modest but functional, with a desk in one corner and papers spread across it in the familiar disorder of a private investigator who is too busy to look organized. The place serves as both home and office: bedroom, workspace, case notes, and clutter all living under the same roof.


He searches the desk first.


Nothing.


He searches again, more carefully this time, turning over papers, opening drawers, checking the places where he would normally leave active case notes.


Still nothing.


The notes on Felda’s job are not in the desk.


He checks his coat next. In one pocket, he finds the envelope she gave him. Inside are ten gold coins, and nothing else.


Then someone knocks on the window.


Not the door.


At the window is a runner wearing the colors of the Gilded Guard. Leo has seen this runner before. He does not speak. He simply hands over a sealed note, then leaves.


Messages like this are not sent through sending-stones or magical channels. When the Gilded Guard wants something secure, they put it in a runner’s hand and have it delivered directly.


Leo sits at his desk and opens the note.


It is handwritten.


E. Voss.
Aether Rail Maintenance Tunnel. Third Junction. Lower Merchant Quarter.
Found this morning. Official cause: harmonic overload. Ministry cleaner on site before the incident report was filed. I am not convinced. The report will be closed by evening. Be covert.


The note bears the seal of M. Bren.


Leo knows that name.


Marshal Bren Sable, of House Sable, is a senior figure in the Gilded Guard. Over the last three years, Leo has worked with him intermittently on four official jobs. Every one of those jobs came through proper channels: a sealed assignment, an authorized payment, and a formal request for private investigative support.


This is different.


Bren has never contacted Leo directly like this before. He has never sent him a personal handwritten note. Leo has never even met him face to face.


The note sits on the desk.


A man named Eren Voss has been found dead in an aether rail maintenance tunnel. The official explanation is harmonic overload: a tragic crystal accident. The report will be closed by evening.


And Bren wants Leo to look into it quietly.


Leo sits in his apartment, holding the note, thinking through what to do next.
Vesper’s First Day in Cyritium
Sixteen days before Ascension Day, the scene shifts to a newly prepared office in Cyritium.


The room smells of fresh plaster and wood oil. By House Aurelian standards, it is not large, but it has been made ready for its new occupant. The space is elegant, almost ethereal: white finishes, gold accents, crystalline details, glassy surfaces, and a few carefully placed plants. It feels less like a government office and more like a refined magical clinic.


The office is arranged as two rooms. The first serves as a reception and work area. The second is a private examination room. On the main desk sits a letter of appointment.


Vesper of Aurelian appointed.
Cyritium liaison. House notary and sanctity compliance.
Effective immediately. Report to the Cyritium estate. An office has been prepared.


Vesper is in her early thirties, elegant, striking, and deliberate in how she presents herself. Her style is refined and expensive, but restrained compared to the grandeur common among Cyritium’s noble circles. Her clothing and accessories echo the celestial divinity of Solaria, shaping her presence into something graceful, devout, and intentionally radiant.


When Vesper first received the office, the first thing she did was perform a religious ceremony inside it. She brought incense, treated the room as a space to be sanctified, and made no effort to hide her devotion. She also brought one object that looked completely out of place among the polished glass, white surfaces, gold accents, and official papers: a plush toy, sitting openly on the desk.


A knock comes at the door.


The door opens, and a young man enters carrying a tray with tea and a sealed envelope. He is younger than he is trying to appear, with cropped hair, a straight spine, and the posture of someone trained for a military posting but placed instead in house administration.


He looks around the room, visibly registering that someone has changed the office.


“Lady Vesper, I am Petre,” he says. “Lord Aurelian’s office asked me to assist you during your transition. I’ll be managing your appointments and correspondence.”


He sets the tray on the desk.


“I’ve prepared a summary of this week’s obligations. There is also an envelope from the Lord’s office directly.”


Vesper tells him to give her the summary.


Petre hesitates. He explains that he was instructed to make sure she reads the envelope before he leaves. It is her first assignment.


Vesper opens it.


Inside is a handwritten note, personal and formal, written in the hand of Lord Aurelian himself. That alone is unusual. Vesper has never received a personal letter from him before.


Welcome to Cyritium.
The calibration today is a formality. Attend the Sun Scouring at the Upper Light Court this evening. I will find you there.


There is no signature, only the house seal.


With the note is a standard assignment sheet for the Festival Crystal Calibration Ceremony. Vesper’s task is to attend, observe, and cosign the compliance verification for forty-six tier-three festival infrastructure crystals.


It is not a difficult assignment. In fact, it appears deliberately simple, the kind of ceremonial duty given to someone being eased into a new position. The Solari engineer will handle the actual calibration. Vesper only needs to observe, verify, and sign.


Petre watches her read it.


He asks whether Lord Aurelian has ever written to her personally before.


Vesper says no.


Petre’s expression tightens slightly. Vesper uses magic to read his surface thoughts.


He wrote to her himself. In three years, he has written to me twice. Both times something was changing.


The thought is not hostile. It is wounded, professional, and carefully buried.


Vesper comforts him, telling him that perhaps it will happen for him one day.


Petre says that in three years, Lord Aurelian has written only twice, and both times the letter was addressed to the office rather than to him personally. Both times, someone was being fired.


He then explains that Vesper should not worry about the assignment. New assignments can be nerve-wracking, but the Solari engineer will handle everything. She only needs to observe, sign, and cosign.


Vesper thanks him and dismisses him.


Petre asks if she needs anything else.


She says no, and tells him to relax.


He insists that he is relaxed.


Then he backs away politely, closes the door, and leaves.
The Crystal Calibration Ceremony
A few hours later, Vesper attends the Festival Crystal Calibration Ceremony.


The ceremony takes place in a hall built for formal procedures: official, controlled, and almost painfully orderly. It is the kind of room designed for witnessing, signing, and moving on. Nothing about it suggests drama. That is the point.


Vesper enters and takes her place among the witnesses.


There are two other observers present. One is a House Halofel compliance witness, carefully recording each step in his own ledger. The other is a young Solari apprentice, clearly attached to the engineer conducting the ceremony. Near the window stands another woman in plain clothes, perhaps in her forties, observing quietly from a slight distance.


Then the engineer enters.


His name is Orvast.


He is a respected House Solari festival engineer, known for having performed this kind of work for decades. It is difficult to place his exact age, but his manner makes one thing clear: he has done this so many times that the ceremony has become muscle memory.


A cart is brought into the center of the room and uncovered.


On it are forty-six tier-three festival infrastructure crystals, arranged in rows on small pedestals. Each crystal is tagged with its inventory number and its assigned destination within the Ascension Festival infrastructure.


These are not everyday lighting crystals. They are the festival crystals, meant to be distributed across the cities of Cyriath for Ascension Day. Each one will play a role in the great ritualized lighting of the beacons.


Orvast carries a small calibration apparatus. He moves from crystal to crystal, touching each one with the tool, listening for the note it produces, and marking his ledger.


The sound is warm, even, and steady.


Every crystal produces the same comfortable hum.


The other witnesses record the process. The Halafel compliance witness takes the duty seriously, writing each result carefully. The Solari apprentice watches closely, as if one day this work will be hers.


Orvast does not look closely at the crystals as he works. He has no need to. Touch, listen, mark. Touch, listen, mark. Each step is clean and efficient.


Then he reaches the last crystal.


He pauses.


Only half a beat, but enough for Vesper to notice.


He taps it again.


This time, he looks at it.


Then instead of making the usual quick mark in his ledger, he writes three short words.


He moves on, closes the ledger, and signs the compliance sheet with a large, practiced signature.


Engineer Orvast of House Solari. Compliance.


He presents the sheet to the witnesses and says:


All verified. Standard stock.


The Halafel witness signs. The woman near the window signs. Then the sheet is brought to Vesper.


Before signing, Vesper quietly casts Detect Thoughts, hiding the spell’s outward signs.


She catches Orvast’s surface thoughts.


This might not be right. I’m not really sure. I’ll take a note and check it later.


The three words he wrote were not a formal objection. They were a private reminder to himself.


Vesper signs, but she does not let the moment pass.


She smiles and says, “It didn’t sound the same as the rest of the crystals, did it?”


Orvast looks at her and smiles back.


“You have an ear for crystals.”


He does not confirm anything.


Then he adds that House Aurelian’s copy will be filed with the festival instruction office by evening.


Vesper presses gently, explaining that she is only curious.


Orvast answers with the polished patience of a man who has been doing this longer than she has been alive.


“I’ve been doing this for the past twenty-eight years. How old are you?”


“Thirty,” Vesper says.


“You were two.”


The correction lands softly, but deliberately.


Then he adds:


Curiosity is one of the merits of the Empire.


As Orvast gathers his things, the young Solari apprentice moves near the crystals and touches them with quiet reverence, as if imagining the day she will be the one performing the ceremony.


Vesper tries to get one more look.


“They look great,” she says. “May I take a look?”


The apprentice glances to Orvast.


Orvast closes the matter.


“We have to go now.”


The cart is covered again. Orvast, the apprentice, and the attendant take the crystals deeper into the Ministry building. Vesper and the other witnesses exit in the opposite direction.


Vesper now knows that one of the forty-six crystals was different.


She also knows that Orvast noticed it.


And he signed anyway.
The Sun Scouring and Lord Aurelian
Later that same day, in the last hour of sunlight, Vesper attends the Sun Scouring at the Upper Light Court.


The Sun Scouring is one of the public acts of faith leading toward Ascension Day. It happens each day in the festival period, as the light begins to fade. The court is designed to catch the last hour of daylight through tilted mirrors and focus it into a warm column of concentrated sun.


As the city outside moves toward evening, the court itself grows brighter.


People come to stand in the light and be cleansed of the old darkness. Some pray to Solaria directly. Others pray to Lunaria, Elidria, or to the Radiant Order as a whole.


The court is tiered. The lower levels are open to ordinary citizens. Families arrive with children, some of whom run and play in the light. The middle levels hold soldiers and lesser nobles. The upper level is reserved for the most elevated guests.


Because Vesper is here to meet Lord Aurelian, she is led upward.


The ceremony is both religious and social. For these few festival moments, the usual boundaries of class soften slightly. Citizens, soldiers, nobles, children, servants, and officials all share the same light, though not from the same height.


Vesper stands in the warm sun and prays.


She prays to Solaria for her daughter, who remains back in Solaris. Her faith is not shaken. If anything, Vesper understands herself as especially chosen. She is not borrowing power in the way a cleric might. She believes Solaria’s power was given to her, personally and directly.


Then Lord Aurelian finds her.


He does not present like the distant head of a noble house. He speaks kindly, with a calm and approachable manner that feels unusual for someone of his rank. He is clearly wealthy, but not dressed in rigid formalwear. He carries authority without making a performance of it.


There is command in him, but not military command.


He feels less like a public nobleman and more like a businessman who found his way to power.


House Aurelian is not among the oldest or grandest houses, and Lord Aurelian is not a major public figure. That gives him more freedom than the heads of the great houses. He is not constantly standing beneath the full glare of the Empire’s attention.


He speaks to Vesper with warmth.


House Aurelian does not hold a higher position in the government. But what we do is very important. Notary is not something to take lightly, especially in our Empire, which runs on checks and balances and faith.


He tells her that if she needs anything, she can send a message through her office, and he will make sure to read it personally.


Then he says:


Again, welcome to Cyritium.


As the exchange ends, Vesper becomes curious about him. Nothing about him openly reveals whether he is a soldier, a mage, or something else. There are no visible signs of power, no obvious uniform, no formal cues that explain exactly what he does or what he commands.


She considers reading his thoughts.


When she does, she realizes something at the same moment.


Lord Aurelian is reading hers.


The exchange is mutual. While Vesper reaches toward his thoughts, he is already listening to hers. He knows she is reading him because he hears the reflection of her own thoughts through the connection.


His surface thought is simple.


Formidable.


He does not react outwardly.


Vesper understands that this is not an act of malice. Among powerful nobles and political figures, thought-reading can be used as a social tool. It is frowned upon, but when done by someone of his rank, it is treated as part of the political landscape. A lord may listen in order to shape the most appropriate response, to keep a meeting smooth, or to make sure the person before him feels seen.


It is not comfortable.


It is also not unusual enough to challenge.


The fact that Lord Aurelian can do this confirms that he is a certified mage, but that does not define his occupation. In this society, magic can be education, status, discipline, or personal cultivation rather than a profession. A person may be trained in magic the way another person might be licensed to carry a weapon.


Everything Vesper knows about him still points to the same public story: Lord Aurelian is a businessman and the head of a minor house.


But now she also knows something else.


He is careful.


He is capable.


And he noticed her noticing him.
Vesper and Leo Compare Notes
The day ends.


After the encounter with Lord Aurelian, Vesper sleeps in the large bed prepared for her in Cyritium. She carries with her the knowledge that Lord Aurelian had been reading her thoughts as naturally as another noble might read a guest’s face.


By morning, it is fourteen days before Ascension Day.


Vesper decides it is time to visit a familiar face.


She knows where to find Leo.


Leo is at his apartment, sitting at his desk and reading the note that arrived from Marshal Bren Sable, when there is a knock at the door.


He puts the note away and opens it.


Vesper is standing outside.


The reunion is warm, familiar, and immediately playful. Leo welcomes her in, compliments her, and the two fall easily into the rhythm of people who already know how to tease each other.


Leo asks how she is doing, and how her new office is.


Vesper tells him the office is great, and that she now understands what it means to be in Cyritium.


Then she asks how his life has been.


Leo admits he has not been doing well. He has been working constantly. Work has become almost everything.


Vesper asks if he has any interesting cases.


Leo says yes. A lot seems to be happening in the city, though he cannot yet say exactly what is wrong. It is more feeling than proof, at least for now.


Vesper presses him.


Leo finally shows her the note from Bren.


E. Voss.
Aether Rail Maintenance Tunnel. Third Junction. Lower Merchant Quarter.
Found this morning. Official cause: harmonic overload. Ministry cleaner on site before the incident report was filed. I am not convinced. The report will be closed by evening. Be covert.


Vesper reads it.


“Ominous,” she says.


She asks who M. Bren is.


Leo explains that Marshal Bren Sable is a good man, and someone he has worked with before. But this is the first time Bren has sent him a handwritten note directly. It is also the first time Bren has asked him to work covertly, off the books.


That makes the message feel serious.


Vesper tells Leo that something strange happened to her as well. She was moved to a new office in Cyritium and assigned to oversee a crystal calibration ceremony.


There were forty-six crystals.


One of them was wrong.


The number catches Leo immediately.


He tells her about Felda and the hidden note she left behind.


The clerk is asking about the forty-sixth line again. It has been handled. Do not involve anyone else.


Vesper explains that during the calibration, the Solari engineer noticed something wrong with one of the crystals. He tried to treat it as nothing, but Vesper caught enough to know he had felt the difference.


Leo asks where the forty-six crystals are now.


Vesper believes they are being sent all across Cyriath. Each city will house one for the coming festival.


The two leads begin to overlap, but not perfectly.


There is Eren Voss, the dead clerk from Bren’s note, officially blamed on harmonic overload in an aether rail maintenance tunnel.


And there is Felda, whose hidden note clearly connects her to the phrase “the forty-sixth line.”


The dead man may not be directly tied to the forty-six crystals.


But Felda almost certainly is.


Leo says they need to speak with Felda.


Vesper asks how he knows her.


Leo explains that Felda hired him. She wanted paperwork done. More specifically, she wanted him to retrieve or switch an original document.


But when Vesper asks what document, Leo realizes he still cannot remember.


He tries to focus.


Vesper helps him, casting guidance and pushing him to recall the details. Leo remembers only fragments: Felda wanted him to go somewhere official, find an original copy, and take it or switch it. The exact place and the exact document keep slipping away.


Again and again, the memory almost surfaces.


Again and again, it vanishes.


It is not ordinary forgetfulness. The detail feels blocked, as if the memory is present but unreachable.


With no clean path back to Felda’s job, they decide to follow Bren’s lead first.


They will go to the Aether Rail Maintenance Tunnel, Third Junction, in the Lower Merchant Quarter, where Eren Voss was found dead.


The location is too far to walk comfortably, so they take an aether cart. The fare is five silver, expensive enough to feel like an indulgence. A normal carriage would cost less, but would take far longer.


They choose speed.


The aether cart carries them through Cyritium toward the Lower Merchant Quarter. The city’s polished face gives way to its working underside.


The Third Junction is not a public destination. It is a service access point into the infrastructure below the city: maintenance tunnels, water systems, sewer lines, and the hidden machinery that keeps Cyritium running.


When they arrive, the entrance is marked with investigation tape.


Investigation ongoing.


The door itself is not locked.


Vesper and Leo look at the entrance, then at each other.


Then they go in.
The Third Junction Investigation
The Tunnel Crime Scene
Vesper and Leo duck under the investigation tape and enter the service access.


The doorway leads down by stairs into the infrastructure beneath Cyritium. This is not an abandoned tunnel. Aether crystals are mounted in sconces along the ceiling, used only for lighting, so their charge lasts a long time. The passage is dirty in the way service spaces are dirty, with scattered tools and signs of maintenance work, but it is clearly still in use.


Their footsteps echo against the stone.


At the bottom of the stairs, they see the body.


A man lies on the tunnel floor, perhaps in his mid-thirties. He wears grey clerk’s robes, not cleric’s robes. He is lying on his side, dead, with blunt trauma visible around the jaw and damage along one hand and arm.


A large scorch mark stains the wall nearby.


Kneeling over the body is a figure dressed entirely in grey. He does not turn when he hears them.


The tunnel is closed. Ministry business.


He says it as if the sentence should be enough to make them leave, then continues his work.


From where they stand, it is not immediately clear whether he is inspecting the body, cleaning the scene, or both. But he appears to be the cleaner Bren warned about: the person sent to clear the crime scene before the incident report is properly filed.


Leo studies the scene from the stairs.


The dead man is a clerk. The right hand shows blue-orange discoloration, consistent with some kind of charged contact exposure. The scorch marks across the floor and wall are not a normal radial blast.


They are directional.


It does not look like the crystal exploded in the victim’s hand and struck the wall.


It looks more like something discharged from a point in the room and hit him.


The cleaner continues working.


Vesper tries to delay him. She casts Suggestion, hiding the spell as much as she can, and tells him that he should check with his superior before removing the body, because the investigators may not have finished their work.


The cleaner turns.


My superior? Of course.


Then he looks at them properly for the first time.


Who authorized your attendance?


At that moment, Vesper quietly casts Detect Thoughts on him.


His surface thoughts begin cataloguing them immediately.


Two people. Female. House-marked. Military bearing.
Male. Unaffiliated. Professional movement pattern.


He is not panicking. He is assessing. The cleaner is trained to catalogue what he sees, and now Vesper and Leo have become part of the scene he is recording.


He asks again who authorized their attendance.


Vesper tries to push deeper into his mind.


The attempt fails.


The cleaner looks directly at her.


What did you find?


Vesper answers smoothly.


“Nothing. You’re good.”


He does not let the matter drop.


So who authorized your attendance?


They improvise.


They avoid using Marshal Bren Sable’s name. Bren asked for discretion, and invoking him here would drag him directly into the scene. Instead, Leo invents an authorization tied to the local Gilded Guard, naming a supposed official called Monsieur Laren.


The cleaner writes it down.


Of which office?


Leo continues the lie, claiming the local Gilded Guard sent them.


The cleaner accepts that as an answer for the moment, but follows procedure.


Can I get your writ? Just for standard purposes.


They do not have one.


Vesper covers the gap by saying they forgot it in the cart. Leo claims it is in his bag and steps out to retrieve it.


Outside, Leo quickly forges a writ.


He has seen crime scene documents before, and he has the tools and skill to imitate one well enough. He writes a document authorizing Mohsen and Alaa, the false names they have chosen, to investigate the scene. To avoid tying it to Bren, he frames the writ through a Lunaris connection rather than anyone directly connected to this case.


It is rushed, but convincing.


While Leo is outside, Vesper remains with the cleaner and keeps him talking.


She asks whether it is strange for something like this to happen in a service tunnel.


The cleaner says it seems like a terrible accident, but people wander. He understands it is upsetting to find, and says he will have the tunnel resealed in about twenty minutes.


Leo returns with the forged writ.


The cleaner takes it, examines it, and gives it back.


Nice to meet you, Alaa and Mohsen.


He logs the names, writes several quick lines into his own records, and returns to his work.


He does not order them out.


The lie has bought them time.


The cleaner resumes cataloguing the body and the scene. His briefcase is open on the ground beside him. One compartment holds evidence bags. Another holds tools. He works with gloved hands and practiced efficiency, preparing to clear the body and close the site.


He asks what official business brought them here.


They say they are double-checking the scene.


The cleaner answers that the investigators were already here in the morning, and that he will have the crime scene closed in about twenty minutes.


He treats the death as already explained.


A harmonic overload.


A terrible accident.


A cleaning job.


But the scene does not match that explanation.


Leo moves closer to the body.


The dead man’s hand and arm are burned or discolored from charged contact, but the blast direction does not match the official cause. His clothing is also strange. He is wearing a heavy wool coat, despite the warm morning.


Why would a clerk be dressed for cold weather in the middle of a warm day?


Leo asks the cleaner whether he has seen something like this before.


The cleaner answers without much concern.


Harmonic overload is quite a nasty business, but it’s rare and happens. Thankfully, this cleaning job will be easy.


The answer is too neat.


The scene is not.


Leo asks to check the man’s pockets.


The cleaner says the morning investigators already searched him, but allows it.


Vesper helps cover Leo while he searches.


In one pocket, Leo finds nothing.


In another, he feels stitching where the pocket lining has been sewn shut. Something is hidden inside, something the earlier investigators missed. With careful hands, he tears the stitching and slips out a folded piece of thick paper.


He hides it.


Continuing the search, Leo checks the coat more thoroughly. Beneath the folded collar, he feels something hard.


A pin.


He cannot examine it openly, but he pockets it as well.

The packet is only a few pages, but dense with legal language. It concerns a formal action between two institutions: a voluntary patent transfer and asset acknowledgement involving House Vermont of Solaris.


The document names House Vermont’s primary commercial asset:


The Vermont Cut


The Vermont Cut is a methodology and license agreement tied to crystal processing. The packet states that all existing crystal inventory processed under that classification is to be transferred to House Aurelian, which is named as the designated Ministry steward.


The stated reason is the arrest proceedings of Lady Lysandra Vermont, on charges of illicit possession and unsanctified divine material.


Vesper has never heard of Lady Lysandra Vermont.

They are identified as designated ward stewards of the Ministry, implying that they are minors or otherwise under Ministry guardianship.

Aurelian co-signature pending. Notarize and return to the Office of Holy Order within three days.

If the Vermont signatures are not yet complete, what exactly is she notarizing?

Lord Aurelian’s signature is already present on the Aurelian side.

House Aurelian, a minor house rather than a major monopoly power, is being named steward of the asset and guardian authority over the Vermont minors.


It is legal.


It is polished.


It is also significant.

Even between minor houses, a transfer of patents, methodology rights, and associated crystal inventory would create noise.
